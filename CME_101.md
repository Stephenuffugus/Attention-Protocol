# CME 101: What You Need to Know to Not Sound Dumb
**Phone-readable. ~5 minutes to read. Read once Sunday or Monday.**

You'll be talking to Directors of Accreditation, VPs of Outcomes, and CME compliance leads at medical-education companies. They live and breathe these terms. You don't have to be an expert. You have to know enough to keep up. This file is the minimum.

---

## What CME actually is

**CME = Continuing Medical Education.** Doctors are required to complete CE credit hours every year to maintain their medical license. Each state and each specialty board has different requirements (typically 20-50 hrs/year). Credit comes from accredited providers in the form of "AMA PRA Category 1 Credit" or specialty equivalents (ABIM-MOC, ABFM, ABPN, etc.).

**CME providers** are the companies that create the educational activities (live conferences, online modules, journal articles, podcasts) and issue the credit. They are accredited by **ACCME** (the regulator).

**MECs = Medical Education Companies.** The independent CME providers, as opposed to academic medical centers or specialty societies. The Top 10 you're targeting are MECs.

**Two revenue models for MECs:**
1. **Direct subscription / fee-per-activity** to physicians (smaller, ~$30-100M revenue companies)
2. **IME grants** from pharma (the bigger pool)

---

## The IME grant cycle (this is THE key concept)

**IME = Independent Medical Education.** This is how pharma funds physician education without controlling content (legally, they cannot direct the curriculum). Pharma companies (Pfizer, Merck, Genentech, AbbVie, BMS) issue **IME grants** to MECs. The MECs design and deliver activities. Doctors learn. Pharma gets a halo, doctors get free CE, and MECs get paid.

**The grant cycle:**
1. Pharma publishes an **RFP** (Request For Proposals) for a specific therapy area (e.g., "metastatic breast cancer 2025-2026")
2. Multiple MECs submit competing proposals
3. Pharma scores them on a rubric and picks 1-3 winners
4. Winners get $250K-$2M typically per grant

**The rubric line that matters for you:**

> "Measurement of learner progression"

Pfizer (and increasingly Merck, BMS, Gilead) has been scoring this section explicitly since 2024. MECs have been scrambling to write it convincingly. Most write platitudes like "we use post-test deltas, NPS scores, and 90-day retention surveys." That's the SAME answer everyone else writes. It doesn't differentiate.

**Your hook is:** SWS lets a MEC write a sentence the others literally cannot:

> *"Each accredited activity emits an Ed25519-signed receipt, Bitcoin-anchored via OpenTimestamps, independently verifiable against our published JWK without calling the vendor."*

That's a paragraph that wins grants. Or at least, that's the bet you're testing in customer-development calls.

## The pressure right now

Three trends pushing CME providers in your direction:

1. **The +73% YoY spike in 21 CFR Part 11 warning letters (H2 2025: 327 warning letters).** Part 11 governs electronic records integrity in regulated settings. Pharma compliance officers are nervous; their nervousness flows to MEC partners.

2. **December 2025 ACCME AI guidance.** ACCME issued guidance on learner integrity in AI-augmented assessments. Translation: ACCME knows physicians are using ChatGPT to fake post-tests. They want providers to think about it. Most providers don't have a coherent answer.

3. **Pfizer's 2024-2025 RFP language.** Pfizer started scoring "measurement of learner progression" explicitly. Other grantors are following. The competitive bar is rising in the rubric, not in the content.

These three trends together = a real wedge for SWS. You're not selling them a product they don't need. You're selling them a paragraph for their next RFP.

## Who to talk to inside a CME provider

Three roles matter, in order:

1. **Director of Accreditation** (or equivalent). Owns the ACCME relationship. Cares about compliance findings, surveyor evidence, learner-integrity story.
2. **VP / Director of Outcomes** (or "Educational Strategy"). Owns the IME grant proposals. Cares about what the rubric demands.
3. **President / Founder** at boutique MECs (single-decision-maker, no procurement layer). At smaller orgs, this person owns everything.

## Things that turn a CME conversation south fast

- **Sounding like behavioral surveillance.** Lead with the no-PII, no-content, no-URLs architecture in the first 60 seconds. CME audiences are privacy-skeptical because their physicians are.
- **Treating physicians as the threat model.** They are not. The threat model is the inattentive learner, the AI-assisted answer, the bot completing CE on a doctor's behalf. Your protocol catches all three. Frame accordingly.
- **Saying "AI" too much.** ACCME isn't anti-AI. They're pro-honesty. Frame SWS as "the cryptographic record that lets you tell ACCME a coherent AI-integrity story" not "the AI tool that catches AI cheaters."
- **Overclaiming on FPR.** Don't say "below 1%." Say "On standard bot profiles the gap is 0.24-0.27. Curve-aware adversarial bots narrow that to 0.13 and the env-gate + composition layers carry the difference. I haven't run it on a real CME population. That's the pilot question."

## Quick check: are you talking to the right person?

If they say "we already do measurement, we use post-tests" -> they are the right person but they're parroting the rubric. Push gently with: "What's been Pfizer's reaction to that answer in the last two RFP cycles?"

If they say "talk to our compliance team" -> they are NOT the right person. Politely accept the handoff but note the conversation as a routing, not a discovery.

If they say "we're Part 11 audited" -> they ARE the right person and they probably have an active pain. Pivot to the +73% YoY warning-letter stat.
