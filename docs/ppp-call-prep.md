# PPP Call Prep — Discovery Conversation Brief

**Purpose:** Everything you need in your head before talking to a Director of Accreditation at Physicians Postgraduate Press. Read it cold once, again the morning of the call, and pull it up on your phone during the call.

**Target outcome of the call:** *not* a pilot. Not a sale. **A learning conversation that ends with them sending a follow-up email or referring you to the right next person.** The pilot is the call after this one.

## 2. Who you are talking to (persona)

### Probable profile

- **Tenure:** 5–15 years in CME publishing or medical education
- **Background:** nursing (RN/MSN), pharmacy (PharmD), or editorial (English/journalism + medical training)
- **Reports to:** VP Education, or directly to PPP's founder/president (small publisher = flat hierarchy)
- **Manages:** small team of 1–4 people
- **Owns:** the ACCME accreditation cycle — paperwork, audits, evidence files
- **Owns:** the response to grantor RFPs in the "measurement of learner progression" section

### What keeps them up at night

1. **ACCME audit risk.** Losing accreditation kills the business, full stop. The December 2025 ACCME AI guidance is fresh, relevant pressure.
2. **Grantor dollar flow.** Psychiatry-CME grants flow primarily from Otsuka, AbbVie, Sumitomo Pharma, Janssen, Lundbeck. RFP "measurement" sections decide who wins. Losing 2–4 mid-size grants per cycle = $400K–$1.5M revenue impact.
3. **Bandwidth.** Tiny team. Every new vendor is a procurement headache they don't have time for.
4. **AI-assisted completion.** *The new fear.* Physicians using ChatGPT to answer post-tests is the conversation IME directors are having privately, that no incumbent vendor solves.

### What they care less about

- Marketing language and "AI-driven" pitches
- Anything that sounds like behavioral surveillance (psychiatry audience is privacy-skeptical — lead with "no PII, no content, no URLs tracked")
- Vendors who "have a team" but no understanding of ACCME mechanics

## 3. The market — 90 seconds of context

### The accreditation chain
```
ACCME (the accreditor)
   ↓ accredits
MECs like PPP, Medscape, DKBmed, Prova
   ↓ propose to
Pharma grantors (Pfizer, Merck, AbbVie, Otsuka, Sumitomo, etc.)
   ↓ fund educational activities for
Physicians (need credits for license renewal — ~50/year, varies by state)
```

### The numbers

- Total US **IME** (Independent Medical Education) spend: roughly **$700M–$1B/year**
- Concentrated in 5–15 large MECs + dozens of small specialty MECs (PPP is in the small-specialty tier)
- A psychiatry MEC typically has 30–80 accredited activities per year
- Mid-size grant: $150K–$750K. Large grant: $1–3M. PPP is mostly mid-size grants.

### Vocabulary you should know

| Term | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **CME** | Continuing Medical Education broadly — any post-graduate medical education | The umbrella term |
| **IME** | Independent Medical Education — pharma-funded accredited CME activities | This is where the dollars are; "independent" means content is editorially independent of the funder |
| **MEC** | Medical Education Company — produces accredited CME | What PPP is |
| **ACCME** | Accreditation Council for Continuing Medical Education | The body that accredits MECs; without their seal you can't issue CME credit |
| **Grantor** | The pharma company funding an IME activity | Pfizer, Merck, Otsuka, AbbVie, etc. They write the RFP and score proposals |
| **RFP** | Request for Proposal | What the grantor sends to invite MECs to compete for a grant |
| **Outcomes** (in CME) | Evidence the activity changed learner behavior | NOT just "did they finish" — Moore's Levels 1-7 framework |
| **Post-test** | Comprehension quiz at the end of an activity | The classic "did they learn it" measure |
| **NPS** | Net Promoter Score — "would you recommend this activity" | A learner-satisfaction proxy |
| **Moore's Levels** | Donald Moore's 7-level framework for CME outcomes (1=participation, 7=community health) | Grantors increasingly want evidence at level 4+ (competence) not just level 1 (attendance) |
| **Measurement of learner progression** | The RFP section evaluating how the MEC will demonstrate learning | This is your wedge — the exact section grantors are scoring harder |
| **Click-through fraud** | Awarding credit to a learner who clicked play and walked away | The classic problem your protocol addresses |
| **AI-assisted completion** | A physician using ChatGPT to answer post-test questions | The new problem — undetected by every incumbent |
| **TECH/MIXED/PROC** | Compliance terminology for who owns a control: technology vendor / joint / procedural-customer-side | Used in your `COMPLIANCE_MATRIX.md` |

### What's broken in their world

Most CME completion = "watched 10 minutes of video, clicked through, got credit." Click-through fraud has always existed. Now AI-assisted completion is the new exposure. Grantors are responding by **scoring "measurement of learner progression" harder**.

The MEC that can write *"per-activity attention-integrity receipt, independently verifiable by your auditor against a public key"* on its proposal **beats** the MEC that writes *"post-test delta and NPS."* That sentence is your wedge.

### Why nobody owns this lane

- **BioCatch / similar** = behavioral fraud detection (banks). Doesn't ship a *signed audit artifact* — just a score that lives in their cloud.
- **Roundtable (YC W26)** = proof-of-humanness scoring API. Same gap — a score, not a receipt.
- **Medscape, Pfizer's internal teams** = no one is producing *cryptographic, offline-verifiable completion receipts* for CME.

You are not selling against an existing solution. You are selling into an **unowned compliance gap** that grantor pressure is opening up right now.

## 5. Questions YOU ask THEM — 10 questions in order

These are **discovery questions**, not pitch traps. Cheap context first, expensive context last. **Listen more than you talk.**

### Cheap context (questions 1–4)

1. **"Walk me through how you currently fill the 'measurement of learner progression' section in an Otsuka or AbbVie psychiatry IME proposal — what does that look like today?"**
   *Why this opening:* tells you exactly how their proposal-writing works and what they currently cite. It is also a softball — they know how to answer it.

2. **"What's changed in grantor expectations on that section in the last 12–18 months?"**
   *Why:* confirms or refutes the Pfizer-2025 / ACCME-Dec-2025 pressure narrative. If they say "nothing has changed," your wedge is wrong for them — but keep going, you'll learn.

3. **"How is your team currently thinking about AI-assisted post-test completion — physicians using ChatGPT to answer the questions?"**
   *Why:* surfaces the AI-assist fear. If they say "we're not worried," they're either lying or unusually relaxed; if they say "this is the conversation we're having privately" — that's your bingo moment.

4. **"When ACCME comes for the next audit, what evidence do you produce that the credits you awarded were earned by real engagement, not click-through?"**
   *Why:* forces them to articulate their current evidence stack. Whatever they list, the honest answer is *"that's procedural evidence, not cryptographic"* — which is your opening.

### Expensive context (questions 5–8)

5. **"Last losing proposal — what did the 'measurement' section look like, and what would the winning version have needed?"**
   *Why:* the most valuable answer in the whole call. Quote in their own words; this becomes your pitch material for the next 5 calls. Many people will not answer this directly. That's fine — even a deflection ("I can't share specifics") tells you something.

6. **"In a typical accredited activity, what % of completers are flagged as suspicious by your current QA process — and what happens to them?"**
   *Why:* gets you their false-positive tolerance, which is the key product question. Your answer to "what's your FPR?" depends on theirs.

7. **"Have you ever evaluated BioCatch, Roundtable, or any 'human verification' vendor? What killed it or kept it alive?"**
   *Why:* names the competitive landscape; their answer tells you what objections to expect.

8. **"Putting aside vendor and integration cost — if I told you tomorrow that every accredited activity emitted a signed cryptographic receipt your auditor could verify offline against our published key, what's the first thing your compliance officer would ask?"**
   *Why:* the hypothetical lets you surface objections without committing to anything. Their first follow-up = your hardest objection to prepare for next call.

### Conversion-track (questions 9–10)

9. **"Where does this conversation go inside PPP if it goes anywhere — who else is in the room before a measurement pilot gets approved?"**
   *Why:* maps the buying committee. Solo Director-of-Accreditation discretion = fast path. CEO-decision-required = 6-month path. You need to know which.

10. **"What would you need to see in the first two weeks of a pilot to know it was worth the next two?"**
    *Why:* the conversion question. If they have an answer, you're talking to a real buyer. If they pause and say *"good question, let me think"* — they don't yet, and that's also useful information. Either outcome moves you forward.

## 7. Things you NEVER say on this call

1. **"We're 99% accurate."** You're not. Sophisticated-bot behavioral gap is small. Lead with the layered receipt instead.
2. **"Our team can have that on your desk Monday."** There is no team. Use *"I"* from sentence one.
3. **"We're already working with several enterprises in your space."** False. They will ask which.
4. **"Don't worry about SOC 2 — nobody really enforces it for pilots."** Procurement does, always.
5. **"We're basically the Stripe for attention."** Horizontal analogies without a beachhead read as unserious.

If you find yourself drifting toward any of these, **stop the sentence.** Silence is fine. Take a breath. Restart from the anti-pitch frame.

## 8. Phrases to keep in your back pocket

When you don't know what to say next, one of these always works:

- **"Say more about that — I want to make sure I'm hearing the actual shape, not the version I assumed."**
- **"That's a different problem than the one I built for. Let me think about that."** *(This is a strength move, not a weakness one. It signals you listen and don't pattern-match.)*
- **"I'd rather your team tell me which of those flagged sessions are real misses than me tell you the model is right."**
- **"I won't pretend I'm at a stage I'm not."**
- **"What would you need to see in the first two weeks of a pilot to know it was worth the next two?"** *(The closer. Use it.)*

## 9. The moment that buys you the follow-up

Per the mock-call transcript in `OUTREACH_PLAY.md §6`: when the Director surprises you with a problem you didn't build for (like *"AI-assisted completion, not bots"*), **your instinct will be to pattern-match it back to your product.** Don't.

Instead: **shut up, ask them to expand, take notes.** *"Say more about that. I want to make sure I understand the actual shape — separate tab, embedded workflow, colleague-on-Slack?"*

That single moment of restraint is what converts a 20-minute polite call into a 45-minute real conversation that ends with them forwarding your email internally.

## 10. Within 30 minutes after they hang up — non-negotiable

From `OUTREACH_PLAY.md §8`. Memory degrades 40% inside an hour. Capture immediately:

1. **Min 0–5:** new file `notes/calls/2026-MM-DD-{name}-PPP.md`. Dump verbatim what they said. Their words, not your paraphrase.
2. **Min 5–10:** extract three deltas — what changed about the product or pitch.
3. **Min 10–20:** draft the follow-up email. Subject and body match exactly what they asked for. Plus ONE line acknowledging what they taught you. **No calendar link. No pilot CTA.** The follow-up IS the next action.
4. **Min 20–25:** log it in your tracker. If next action is "send three docs by Friday," it goes on Friday's calendar **today**.
5. **Min 25–30:** **do not call the next prospect.** You're amped. You'll mis-pitch. Walk away from the desk for ten minutes. Then start whatever the call surfaced as the next artifact.

Hit all three of: captured note, delta to product/pitch, same-day delivery commitment. Miss any one and the call was tourism.

You hardened it through seven rounds of hostile review plus a 2026-05-07 production-tightening pass (~90 findings closed, attacker cost up 100–400×). You submitted YC. The protocol does what you say it does. The market gap is real. The grantor pressure is real. The patent priority date is real.
