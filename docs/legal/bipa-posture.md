# SWS Biometric-Statute Legal Posture (BIPA / TX SB 800 / WA HB 1493 / CPRA SPI)

**Status:** Internal working draft — not legal advice. Authored 2026-04-28 in
response to the round-2 hostile-review legal agent's finding that the
Compliance Matrix said "Biometric: NO" while several marketing surfaces
self-described SWS as "behavioral biometrics." This document reconciles the
two and states the legal theory under which SWS does not believe it collects
"biometric identifiers" as those terms are defined in current state statutes.

**Pending counsel review** before any pilot signs. Do not present this
document as a legal opinion to a customer or counterparty without express
counsel sign-off.

## The reconciled story (what we'll say in marketing + contracts)

SWS measures **aggregate behavioral signals** during an attention-tracked
session. Specifically, the SDK computes 23 numeric statistics over the
session — Fitts' Law movement-time compliance, Hick's Law decision-time
compliance, prewhitened DFA-1 1/f scaling on keystroke inter-event series,
two-thirds power law on cursor curvature, reading-speed coherence per
section, keystroke digraph-class timing distributions, micro-pause cadence,
submovement count per Crossman-Goodeve / Meyer 1988, microsaccade
detection per Engbert-Kliegl 2003, and 1/f cross-channel coherence.

The protocol does **not** collect:

- retina or iris scans
- fingerprints
- voiceprints
- scans of hand geometry
- scans of face geometry
- raw keystroke event streams (privacy-safe class bucketing only — keys
  are bucketed into letter / number / space / punct / modifier / nav / enter
  / other; the actual character is never recorded)
- cleartext written content (the reflection text is SHA-256-hashed
  client-side before any persistence)

## Why this is not a "biometric identifier" under BIPA §10

Illinois BIPA §10 (740 ILCS 14/10) defines "biometric identifier" as:

> "a retina or iris scan, fingerprint, voiceprint, or scan of hand or face
> geometry"

— and explicitly excludes (among other things) "physical descriptions such
as height, weight, hair color, or eye color" and "writing samples, written
signatures, photographs, human biological samples used for valid scientific
testing or screening, demographic data, [or] tattoo descriptions."

The SDK's 23 signals are **aggregate temporal-statistical descriptions of
voluntary motor and cognitive activity**. They are not scans of any of the
five enumerated identifier types. The closest analogy in statute is "writing
sample" (which is excluded) — except SWS doesn't store the writing itself
either.

This reading is shared by the public posture of behavioral-analytics
companies that operate in Illinois without §15 consent flows (e.g.,
session-replay vendors collecting mouse-movement data have generally not
been treated as collecting BIPA-regulated identifiers, though the case law
is unsettled — see Bryant v. Compass Group, Cothron v. White Castle, and
the open class-action docket as of 2025).

**Caveat:** The case law is moving against this reading. Plaintiffs'-bar
class actions have argued behavioral-biometric vendors collect identifiers
under §10 by analogy. Counsel should confirm SWS's posture survives
*Bezar*-class arguments before the first IL/TX/WA pilot. The lighter
remediation path (§15(a) public retention/destruction schedule + §15(b)
written release) is also viable; this document documents path (a).

## Why this is not a "biometric identifier" under TX SB 800 (Texas Capture
or Use of Biometric Identifier — Bus. & Com. Code §503.001)

Texas defines "biometric identifier" as:

> "a retina or iris scan, fingerprint, voiceprint, or record of hand or face
> geometry"

— substantially similar to BIPA. The same analysis applies. The 2023-25
amendments (eff. 9/1/2023, $25K/violation, AG enforcement) materially
expanded enforcement risk but did not materially expand the §503.001
definition. SB 800 also does not include keystroke or mouse-trajectory
statistics in the enumerated types.

## Why this is not a "biometric identifier" under WA HB 1493 (RCW 19.375)

Washington's HB 1493 covers "biometric identifier" defined as:

> "data generated by automatic measurements of an individual's biological
> characteristics, such as a fingerprint, voiceprint, eye retinas, irises,
> or other unique biological patterns or characteristics that is used to
> identify a specific individual"

The Washington statute is broader than BIPA/TX in including "other unique
biological patterns or characteristics ... used to identify a specific
individual." The SWS posture under WA depends on the **identification**
prong: the protocol does not maintain per-user behavioral profiles for
identification. Each session generates a fresh anonymous UID; there is
no cross-session linkability of behavioral signals (the SDK does not
fingerprint a returning user). Under the WA statute's identification-
purposes prong, aggregate per-session statistics that are not used to
identify a returning individual likely fall outside §19.375.

Counsel should confirm. If WA enforcement risk is material, the lighter
path (§15-style consent flow at session start) is viable.

## CPRA Sensitive Personal Information (Cal. Civ. Code §1798.140(ae))

California's CPRA expanded SPI to include "personal information collected
and analyzed concerning a consumer's biometric information." If a
California regulator were to read "biometric information" broadly to
include behavioral-temporal statistics, SWS would fall under SPI.

**Mitigation in product**:
1. The CPRA Limit-Use mechanism (§1798.121) is implemented at the consent
   flow (a "Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information" toggle).
2. Sec-GPC global privacy control is honored.
3. No cross-session profiling is performed; each session is an island.
4. Residual data is the receipt — a content-bound hash, not the underlying
   signal stream.

Counsel should review the CPRA-specific language before any California
B2B2C deployment (a CME provider serving California-licensed HCPs).

## GDPR Article 9 special category data

The aggregate behavioral signals are not "data revealing racial or ethnic
origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, or trade
union membership" or "genetic data, biometric data for the purpose of
uniquely identifying a natural person, data concerning health" etc.
GDPR Art. 9(1) is not implicated.

GDPR Art. 22 (automated individual decision-making) IS implicated for any
EU user when SWS is used as a gate on CME credit. Mitigation:

- Art. 22(2)(c) consent toggle in the consent flow for EU IPs
- Art. 13(2)(f) per-signal explanation panel surfaced on the receipt
- Art. 22(3) human-review path: the MARGINAL/FAIL stopgap mailto in
  cme-demo.html (T2-8 review queue is the proper long-term fix)

## Honest residual risks

1. **Class-action plaintiffs' bar.** BIPA class certification is routinely
   granted post-Cothron. Even with this defensible reading, an IL plaintiff
   firm can file and the litigation cost (defense + insurance retention)
   exceeds the pilot's gross margin. Mitigation: cyber + E&O insurance
   (queued T1-5), and the lighter §15(a)/(b) consent flow as an alternative
   posture if litigation pressure materializes.

2. **State AG sweeps.** TX AG and CA CPPA have both run biometric-vendor
   sweeps in 2024-25. Mitigation: tight, written posture + EU-style
   consent flow + insurance.

3. **CPRA enforcement reading.** California's regulator has not yet
   published guidance on whether behavioral-temporal statistics fall under
   the SPI definition. Mitigation: implement Limit-Use + Sec-GPC at
   product level so the question is academic.

4. **Posture vs. industry self-marketing.** The "behavioral biometric"
   industry term (BioCatch, BehavioSec, NuData) is widely used. SWS
   self-marketing has previously used the term. Round-2 hardening strips
   "behavioral biometric" from SWS's own product surfaces; the term is
   retained only when describing competitors (factual references in
   COMPETITIVE_INTELLIGENCE.md, YC_S26_APPLICATION_DRAFT.md, etc.).
