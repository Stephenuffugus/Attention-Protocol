1. Epidemiology & 2025 Guideline Update

Hypertension affects 47.7% of U.S. adults (CDC 2024, updated 2025). The 2025 AHA/ACC guidance reaffirmed Stage 1 Hypertension at 130/80 mmHg and Stage 2 at 140/90 mmHg, but substantially elevated the role of home blood pressure monitoring (HBPM) over in-office measurement alone.

White-coat hypertension and masked hypertension both now carry A-level evidence recommendations for HBPM-based confirmation, particularly in patients aged 65 and older. Before initiating therapy in a newly-diagnosed Stage 1 case, practitioners are now expected to document at least one week of HBPM readings unless the patient has compelling comorbidities (established cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, diabetes mellitus).

2. First-Line Therapy Selection

Four first-line drug classes remain: thiazide diuretics, calcium-channel blockers (CCBs), angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEi), and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs). Selection is guided by patient demographics and comorbid profile.

For Black patients without compelling comorbidities, thiazide or CCB monotherapy is preferred over ACEi/ARB due to differences in response rate. For patients with diabetes and albuminuria, ACEi or ARB is first-line regardless of demographic profile — the renal-protective effect dominates. Chronic kidney disease stage 3 introduces dose-adjustment considerations and, at stage 4-5, may shift agent choice away from ACEi/ARB toward alternatives.

3. Comorbid Conditions & Dose Adjustment

In heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), the foundation remains ACEi or ARB plus beta-blocker plus aldosterone antagonist, now augmented by SGLT2 inhibitors per 2024 HFA guidelines. In CKD stage 3-5, avoid potassium-sparing diuretics without frequent electrolyte monitoring.

In elderly patients (age ≥ 75), initial dose reduction is standard practice to mitigate orthostatic events. In pregnancy, labetalol, nifedipine, and methyldopa remain the safe, evidence-supported choices; ACEi and ARB are contraindicated in all trimesters.

4. Patient Education & Adherence

Medication adherence in hypertension is approximately 50% at one year without structured intervention. Evidence-based adherence strategies include: once-daily dosing when possible, simplified regimens, teach-back counseling during visits, home BP logs reviewed at each clinical encounter, and phone-based between-visit check-ins during the first 90 days of a new regimen.

Lifestyle counseling should emphasize the DASH dietary pattern, sodium reduction to <2.3g/day, and 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise unless contraindicated. Weight reduction of 5-10% in obese patients produces clinically significant BP reduction independent of pharmacotherapy.
